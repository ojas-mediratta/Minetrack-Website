Introduction & Background

Estimating motion from visual input is a foundational challenge in robotics, computer vision, and autonomous systems. Visual Odometry (VO) seeks to recover an agent's trajectory from a sequence of images by estimating the relative transformation between consecutive frames. By composing these incremental transformations, a full trajectory can be reconstructed. Unlike full SLAM systems, visual odometry focuses purely on motion estimation and does not maintain a persistent global map or perform loop closure correction. Nevertheless, accurate VO remains a critical component of modern navigation systems.

In this project, we propose MineTrack, a learning-based visual odometry framework implemented in Minecraft. Minecraft provides a controllable 3D environment with precise ground-truth pose data, allowing reproducible evaluation of motion estimation performance. MineTrack adopts a two-part learning strategy:

  • An unsupervised [whatever unsupervised preprocessing method we decide on] learns compact latent embeddings of gameplay frames.
  • A supervised neural network predicts relative motion parameters from these embeddings.

By decoupling representation learning from pose regression, MineTrack investigates whether unsupervised visual embeddings can encode sufficient geometric structure to support accurate trajectory reconstruction. Predicted transformations are sequentially composed and compared against ground-truth motion logs to evaluate drift and reconstruction error.

Motivation

Motivation: While conducting SLAM in Minecraft is an impressive project in itself, our main motivation for this task is the extrapolation for SLAM in higher-fidelity virtual and physical environments.
Minecraft serves as a fully controllable testbed for our supervised and unsupervised testing, as there are procedurally generated terrains with large-scale 3D spaces and dynamic lighting conditions.
Compared to the real world, Minecraft provides perfect map data and ground truth poses, adjustable noise models, and repeatable simulation testing in an infinite and stochastic world.
By training machine learning models to conduct SLAM in Minecraft, we can aim for eventual deployment in higher-fidelity virtual worlds and the physical world.
Models validated in Minecraft can be transferred to photorealistic simulators with more realistic physics engines and sensor modules, with the ultimate goal being SLAM in real-world environments.

Methods

Approach Overview

We aim to solve a visual odometry (VO) problem formulated as regression of image-to-image transforms from sequences of monocular RGB input images. We propose a two-stage encoder-regressor model to produce vector embeddings from input images and transform outputs from embeddings.

Machine Learning Algorithms

CNN encoders are well-studied and effective for odometry . The first stage in the proposed architecture, the CNN encoder, ingests RGB input images and produces a series of lower-dimensional embedding vectors, one per image. We plan to train this encoder as an autoencoder, using the reconstruction error from converting the embeddings back into raw images using a second CNN. The second stage uses an MLP, RNN, or SVM to regress an output transform. The MLP approach takes two embedding vectors from each sequential pair of images and estimates a 3D transform between them. The RNN takes in a series of embedding vectors and uses its own state to produce a series of output transforms. Finally, the SVM takes concatenated pairs of embedding vectors and uses a hyperplane in their combined vector space to regress a transform output. Vision transformers (ViTs) have also been shown to be effective at visual odometry due to their inclusion of vision encoders and handling multiple images without time-dependence like RNNs . Therefore, we also propose a modified end-to-end ViT as an alternative to the encoder-regressor pipeline. This model would use patch embedding on input images and a transformer encoder with a pose regression head to produce output transforms.
